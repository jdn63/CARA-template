CARA Methodology

How risk scores are calculated and what they mean

PHRAT Formula

All domain scores are combined using the PHRAT (Public Health Risk Assessment Tool) quadratic mean formula:

Total Score = sqrt( sum( weight_i x score_i^2 ) )

Each domain score is on [0, 1]. Domain weights are configured per profile and optionally per jurisdiction. The quadratic mean penalizes concentrated risk in a single domain more than a linear mean would, which better reflects the amplifying effect of compound vulnerabilities in public health emergencies.

The final composite score is also on [0, 1] and maps to four risk tiers: Low (0.00-0.39), Moderate (0.40-0.59), High (0.60-0.74), and Critical (0.75-1.00).

EVR Framework

Each domain score is computed using the EVR (Exposure-Vulnerability-Resilience) framework, which reflects the internationally accepted hazard risk model described in the Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction and WHO IHR guidance:

  • Exposure - the degree to which a population encounters a hazard (event frequency, concentration, proximity)
  • Vulnerability - the susceptibility of that population to harm (demographic factors, chronic disease burden, social determinants)
  • Resilience - the capacity of the system to absorb, adapt, and recover (health infrastructure, preparedness, mitigation investment)

A health impact factor is applied to domains where data on direct health outcomes (excess mortality, ED visits, displacement) is available.

Assessment framework: {{ framework_name | default('Not configured') }}

{% if profile == 'international' or framework_key == 'who_ihr' %}

Action plans and capability assessments are aligned with the WHO International Health Regulations (IHR 2005) core capacities. Each action plan item is tagged to one of the 19 IHR core capacities and, where applicable, to the corresponding Joint External Evaluation (JEE) indicator.

The IHR core capacities cover: legislation and policy, coordination and EOC, zoonotic disease, food safety, laboratory, surveillance, reporting, point of entry, chemical events, radiation emergencies, risk communication, human resources, preparedness, emergency response, linking public health to security authorities, medical countermeasures, and means of deployment.

{% else %}

Action plans and capability assessments are aligned with the CDC Public Health Emergency Preparedness (PHEP) capabilities. Each action plan item is tagged to one of the 15 CDC PHEP core capabilities.

The 15 PHEP capabilities are: Community Preparedness, Community Recovery, Emergency Operations Coordination, Emergency Public Information and Warning, Fatality Management, Information Sharing, Mass Care, Medical Countermeasure Dispensing, Medical Materiel Management, Medical Surge, Non-Pharmaceutical Interventions, Public Health Laboratory Testing, Public Health Surveillance, Responder Safety and Health, and Volunteer Management.

{% endif %}

Limitations

  • CARA scores represent relative risk within the jurisdiction set and are not absolute measures of hazard probability or expected loss.
  • Data availability and recency vary by connector and jurisdiction. Scores for jurisdictions with limited monitoring data should be interpreted cautiously.
  • The PHRAT formula and domain weights represent policy choices and should be reviewed and validated by public health officials familiar with the jurisdiction.
  • CARA does not replace clinical, epidemiological, or operational judgment. It is a decision support tool, not a decision-making tool.
